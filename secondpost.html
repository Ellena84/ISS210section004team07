Book banning is a violation students of First Amendment rights

The classroom is often a student’s first exposure to values and morality that impact the way they interact with American society. The supreme court has ruled that students have diminished first amendment rights but has also held that a freedom to express ideas is also a freedom to receive those ideas. The United States supreme court has only weighed in on book banning in schools once, no firm precedent was reached for lower courts to draw on, allowing schools to continue banning books that are “vulgar, not educationally suitable” and did not move to define those terms. The determination of what is vulgar is left to school boards based on “community values."

In this photo, Gail Sheehy, author of "Passages," at podium, right, reads during the "First Banned Books Read Out" in New York, April 1, 1982. The rally protested censorship by school and public libraries of certain books under pressure from right wing religious groups.

Book banning is disproportionatly affecting BIPOC communities

The American Library Association shows that banned books are disproportionately written by LGBTQ and BIPOC authors or books discussing LGBTQ subjects and race. Seemingly “objective” standards for academic achievement align with white normativity and force BIPOC students to either abandon or modify their identities or face the risk of being deemed academically deficient and left behind. The use of white cultural values when evaluating books silences the BIPOC community and erases representation.
